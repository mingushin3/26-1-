Malignancy is driven by genetic and epigenetic alterations within specific cells, enabling them to grow, 
survive, and proliferate independently of the surrounding microenvironment
•Self-Sufficiency in Growth: Cancer cells produce their own growth factors (e.g., TGF, PDGF) to drive 
proliferation without external signals.
•Oncogenic Drivers: Mutations such as KRAS become constitutively active, sending constant, self-driven 
growth signals within the cell.
•Intrinsic Resistance: Mechanisms to evade cell death or resist therapy are encoded directly within the cancer 
cell, rather than being solely provided by the surrounding stroma.
•Metabolic Reprogramming: Cells alter their metabolism to sustain high growth rates independently of 
neighboring cells.
Cell-autonomous view of cancer

Why does drug resistance recur?
1) Genetic Alteration
- Cancer cells acquire secondary mutation (e.g., imatinib resistance due to BCR-ABL kinases)
2) Drug Efflux / Drug Inactivation 
- Overexpression of ABC transporters can actively pump out chemo drugs 
3) DNA Repair System & Escape from Cell Death 
4) Tumor microenvironment
- Stromal cells and cytokines within the tumor microenvironment can provide survival signals that protect cancer
cells from therapy
5) Epigenetic Changes and Phenotypic Plasticity
6) Tumor heterogeneity
- Pre-existing resistant subclones within a heterogeneous tumor can expand under therapeutic pressure
